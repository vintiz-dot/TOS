Overarching Unit Goal(s) Students will develop an understanding of the biological lifecycle of humans—from the fetal stage through elderly—while building English language proficiency in scientific contexts. They will recognize that human development involves both physical (visible body changes) and mental (invisible brain/cognitive changes) growth, and apply this understanding to their own lived experiences.

3. Learning Objectives (Bloom's Taxonomy — Backward Design)

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

Bloom's Level Learning Objective
REMEMBER 1. List the four main stages of human development: infancy, childhood, adolescence, and adulthood.
2. Recall the English terms and pronunciations for vocabulary words (foetus, toddler, puberty, adolescence, infancy, childhood, adulthood, elderly, teenager).
UNDERSTAND 3. Identify the correct life stage corresponding to specific age ranges (e.g., 0–1 year = infancy).
4. Explain the difference between physical changes (to the body) and mental changes (to how the brain works).
APPLY 5. Classify people into the correct developmental stage based on visual cues or written descriptions (e.g., "Mason is 8 → Childhood").
ANALYZE 6. Organize the stages of human growth into the correct chronological order, from baby to elderly, justifying the sequence.
EVALUATE 7. Assess whether given statements about human development are true or false based on reading comprehension, citing specific text evidence to defend their claim.
CREATE 8. Formulate predictions about how their own body and mind will develop over the next ten years, distinguishing between predicted physical and mental changes.

4. Formative Assessments

Objective(s) Assessed Assessment Type Description Data Usage & Adaptive Scaffolding
REM ANL Concept Sort (Collaborative) Students physically sort vocabulary cards into chronological order, matching terms to age ranges and images. Scaffold: If struggling, provide visual icons next to terms and pre-grouped age ranges. Teacher circulates to catch misconceptions early.
UND APP RallyRobin Classification Partners take turns classifying projected scenarios (images + text) into life stages, identifying physical vs. mental traits. Scaffold: Provide bilingual glossary cheat-sheet for A2 learners. Model first scenario as "We Do."
EVL Claim & Defend (T/F) Students evaluate T/F statements using the textbook passage (p. 40), underlining the exact sentence that proves their answer. Scaffold: Highlight key paragraphs in the text. Provide sentence frame: "This is [true/false] because the text says '______'."
CRE Mini-PBL: "Future Me" Profile Students create a profile predicting 2 physical and 2 mental changes over 10 years. Choice of writing, drawing, or audio recording. Scaffold: Sentence starters provided (e.g., "In ten years, my body will..." / "In ten years, my brain will..."). Graphic organizer template.

5. Big Ideas, Vocabulary & Prior Knowledge

Big Ideas / Essential Questions Big Idea: Growing up involves both visible (physical) and invisible (mental) changes that follow predictable patterns but vary by individual.

Provoking Question: "If your body stops growing taller when you are an adult, do you stop developing?"

Guiding Questions:
  • How is a toddler different from an adolescent?
  • What changes will happen to you in the next 10 years?
  • Can you name changes you can see vs. changes you can't?
Target Academic Language & Vocabulary Tier 3 Science Terms:
Foetus /ˈfiː.təs/ • Infancy /ˈɪn.fən.si/ • Toddler /ˈtɒd.lər/ • Childhood /ˈtʃaɪld.hʊd/ • Adolescence /ˌæd.əˈles.əns/ • Puberty /ˈpjuː.bə.ti/ • Teenager /ˈtiːn.eɪ.dʒər/ • Adulthood /ˈæd.ʌlt.hʊd/ • Elderly /ˈel.də.li/

Tier 2 Academic Terms:
Development, physical changes, mental changes, stage, growth, lifecycle
Prior Knowledge Required
  • Basic family/age vocabulary (baby, child, adult, old person)
  • Concept of growing up from personal experience
  • Basic ability to read short English passages (A2 level)
  • Experience with pair/group work protocols
Anticipated Student Misconceptions
Misconception 1: "Development only means getting taller/bigger."
Correction: Explicitly teach and contrast physical vs. mental/cognitive changes throughout the lesson.
Misconception 2: "Puberty happens at the exact same age for everyone."
Correction: Emphasize that adolescence and puberty have a range (usually 12–19) and vary by individual.
Misconception 3: "Elderly people stop developing."
Correction: Highlight that mental development (wisdom, learning) continues throughout life.

6. Learning Theories

Selected Learning Theories & How They Guide This Lesson Constructivism (Piaget) Social Constructivism (Vygotsky) Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller) Inquiry-Based Learning

Constructivism (Piaget): Students build on existing schemas about aging (they know "baby" and "old person") by accommodating new, specific scientific terms into their understanding. The Concept Sort activity requires active construction of knowledge rather than passive reception, aligning with Piaget's emphasis on schema construction through assimilation and accommodation.

Social Constructivism (Vygotsky): Collaborative activities (RallyRobin, Concept Sort, group sharing) operate within the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD). Peer interaction allows A2 learners to achieve classification and evaluation tasks they could not accomplish independently. The structured Kagan protocols ensure equitable participation and scaffold social learning.

Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller): Given A2 English constraints, the lesson minimizes extraneous cognitive load by presenting vocabulary visually with paired images, using minimal on-slide text, and scaffolding progressively through Bloom's levels. The physical/mental distinction uses consistent color coding to reduce split-attention effects.

Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL): The See-Think-Wonder thinking routine transforms students from passive recipients into active questioners. By generating their own wonderings about human development before receiving instruction, students develop intrinsic motivation and ownership of learning.

7. Student Diversity & Differentiation of Instruction

Group Group Diversity Differentiation of Instruction
Group A Lower-proficiency ELL (A1–A2 border) Scaffolding
  • Provide bilingual (English/Vietnamese) vocabulary cards with images for the Concept Sort.
  • Sentence frames for all production tasks: "A [vocabulary word] is a person who is [age] years old."
  • Pair with stronger English peers (strategic heterogeneous pairing).
  • Pre-highlight key sentences in the reading passage for the T/F evaluation task.
Group B On-level A2 learners Standard Path
  • Full participation in all activities as designed.
  • Access to vocabulary cards (English only) with images.
  • Expected to write original sentences and complete T/F with text evidence independently.
Group C Advanced / Fast finishers Extension
  • Extension challenge during Concept Sort: Add the "Fetal" stage and justify its placement before infancy.
  • During "Future Me" PBL: Add a third category of social/emotional changes to their predictions.
  • Peer tutoring role during collaborative activities — explain concepts to Group A in simplified English.

8. Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

UDL Principle Implementation in This Lesson
Multiple Means of Representation
  • Time-lapse aging video (visual/auditory)
  • Textbook passage with printed definitions (text)
  • Color-coded physical vs. mental change system (visual scaffold)
  • Vocabulary cards with images + pronunciation guides (multimodal)
Multiple Means of Engagement
  • See-Think-Wonder hook generates student curiosity and ownership
  • Kagan RallyRobin protocol ensures equal participation and lowers affective filter
  • Choice in PBL output format (write, draw, or record audio)
  • Relevance: "Future Me" connects scientific content to students' personal identity
Multiple Means of Action & Expression
  • Physical card sorting (kinesthetic expression)
  • Verbal partner discussions (oral expression)
  • Written T/F with text evidence (literacy expression)
  • Choice board for "Future Me": write, draw labeled diagrams, or record 30-sec audio clips

IBL Thinking Routine 		Provocation: Project a 60-second time-lapse aging video (person aging 0 → 70 years). Students watch silently.

Thinking Routine:
"What do you see changing?" (Physical observations)
"What do you think is changing inside their brain?" (Mental inference)

Record 3–4 student wonders on the board. Revisit at lesson close.
State learning objectives immediately after (Slide 3).
🔑 IBL Connection: Student-generated questions drive subsequent instruction. This transforms the lesson from teacher-telling to student-discovering.
 I Do → We Do
8–20 Discovery & Concept Sort
IBL Differentiation UDL 		Vocabulary Introduction (4 min, I Do): Present 9 terms using vocabulary slides (Slides 4–6). Model pronunciation for each term. Students choral repeat, then individual spot-checks.

Key Distinction (2 min): Explicitly teach physical vs. mental changes using the split visual (Slide 4). Address Misconception 1 directly: "Development is NOT just getting bigger."

Concept Sort (6 min, We Do → You Do): Distribute card sets (9 vocab words, 9 images, 9 age ranges) to groups of 4. Instead of giving definitions, students analyze and logically group the cards by matching terms to ages and images, then arrange in chronological order.
Teacher circulates to monitor, probe with guiding questions, and correct.
🔀 Tech Integration: If interactive whiteboard available, one group completes the Concept Sort digitally on the board while others use physical cards.
⚠️ Misconception Check: Watch for students placing "Puberty" as a separate stage rather than a process within Adolescence. Address by saying: "Puberty is not a stage—it's something that happens during adolescence."
 I Do → We Do → You Do
20–30 Application: RallyRobin
Kagan Structure Differentiation 		Classify & Apply (Slide 7): Project real-world scenarios (e.g., "Mason is 8, goes to school, has lots of energy" / "Hannah is married with 3 children, works as a teacher").

RallyRobin Protocol: Partners take turns:
Partner A classifies the person's life stage → Partner B states whether the description highlights a physical or mental trait → Switch roles for next scenario.

Project 4–5 scenarios of increasing complexity. Final scenario: "Your grandfather is 72, tells amazing stories, but his hair is gray and he walks slowly." Students must identify BOTH physical AND mental traits.
👥 Strategic Pairing: Group A (lower-proficiency) students paired with Group B/C peers. Provide Group A with a reference glossary during this activity.
 We Do → You Do
30–40 Evaluation: Claim & Defend
Critical Thinking 		Reading Comprehension (Slide 8): Students independently read the "Human Development" passage (textbook p. 40).

T/F Evaluation with Evidence:
1. "Babies don't look like their parents." — T / F
2. "Physical changes are changes to our bodies." — T / F
3. "Babies need to learn how to feed themselves." — T / F
4. "Children's brains work the same way as a baby's brain." — T / F

Higher-Order Requirement: Students must underline the exact sentence in the text that proves their T/F answer. This pushes beyond simple recall into evidence-based evaluation.

Review answers as whole-class discussion. Call on students to read their evidence aloud.
💡 Discussion Prompt: "Who found a different sentence to prove their answer? Can you have different evidence for the same answer?"

PBL UDL Differentiation 		Creating (Slide 9): "You are approximately 10 years old (Childhood). In 10 years, you will be 20 (entering Adulthood). What will change?"

• Write/Draw 2 physical changes ("How will your body look different?")
• Write/Draw 2 mental changes ("How will your brain think differently?")

Option B: Labeled illustration/diagram

10. 21st Century Knowledge & Skills (5C's)

21st Century Skill Teaching Strategies / Activities
Critical Thinking See-Think-Wonder routine (generating scientific observations and inferences); Claim & Defend T/F evaluation (evidence-based reasoning); Concept Sort (analyzing and organizing information logically).
Communication Choral and individual pronunciation practice; RallyRobin partner verbalization; "Future Me" class presentation sharing; discussion of text evidence.
Collaboration Kagan RallyRobin structured partner work; group Concept Sort activity; peer review during "Future Me" creation; strategic heterogeneous pairing.
Creativity Mini-PBL "Future Me" — students synthesize scientific knowledge into personal creative predictions using writing, drawing, or audio. Open-ended product with no single "correct" answer.
Citizenship Respectful active listening during peer sharing; English-only classroom agreement; understanding human diversity in developmental timelines (not everyone develops at the same age).

11. Science & Engineering Practices (SEPs)

Science & Engineering Practice Teaching Strategies / Activities
Asking Questions & Defining Problems See-Think-Wonder routine — students generate scientific questions about human development after observing the time-lapse video provocation.
Developing & Using Models Concept Sort cards serve as a physical model of the human lifecycle sequence. Students arrange, rearrange, and justify their model.
Analyzing & Interpreting Data Students analyze age ranges, visual descriptions, and textbook data to classify individuals into developmental stages (RallyRobin).
Engaging in Argument from Evidence Claim & Defend T/F — students must cite specific textual evidence to support or refute claims about human development. Class discussion of conflicting evidence.
Obtaining, Evaluating & Communicating Information Students read scientific text, extract developmental data, construct original sentences, and communicate predictions via the "Future Me" PBL product.
